What on Earth is Morse Code?

A crash course for the curious, the bored, and the mildly paranoid.

A painter named Samuel Morse came up with it in the 1830s. Yes, a painter. He was sailing home from Europe when he heard someone mention electromagnetic telegraphs — and thought, "I could do that." Iconic energy.

His assistant Alfred Vail did a lot of the heavy engineering lifting, but history remembered Morse's name. Classic.

Fun fact

Morse originally imagined the receiver printing out a squiggly line on paper tape — not dots and dashes at all. Operators quickly realised they could just listen to the clicks instead. Oops, genius.

Every letter and number is turned into a unique pattern of dots (short beeps) and dashes (long beeps). That's it. That's the whole system.

S · · · three short beeps
O — — — three long beeps
SOS · · · — — — · · · the world's most famous signal

You can send Morse via sound, light, taps, blinks — basically anything that can be "on" or "off." Even blinking your eyes. (People have actually done this. More on that below.)

Here's the twist — it doesn't. SOS doesn't stand for anything. It was chosen in 1905 purely because · · · — — — · · · is dead simple to send and impossible to misread in a panic. "Save Our Souls" came later as a backronym because humans love a good story.

Plot twist

The Titanic initially sent CQD — the old distress signal meaning "All stations: Distress." A junior operator suggested switching to the new SOS. The Titanic became one of the first ships to use it. It did not help as much as hoped.

Glad you asked. Here are three genuinely wild ones:

1966 — Jeremiah Denton, POW

An American pilot held captive in Vietnam was forced to appear in a propaganda video. While speaking, he blinked the word T·O·R·T·U·R·E in Morse code. The US Navy decoded it. The world found out.

1912 — RMS Titanic

Titanic's distress signals brought the RMS Carpathia to the rescue. Without Morse, the 705 survivors might not have made it either.

1995 — The movie "Ace Ventura"

Okay not life-saving. But Jim Carrey's wacky Morse scene introduced a generation of kids to the concept. We're counting it.

More than you'd think! It was officially retired from maritime use in 1999, but it lives on in:

📻 Ham Radio Hobbyists still use it worldwide — it punches through interference that voice can't
✈️ Aviation Airport beacons still broadcast their ID codes in Morse
♿ Accessibility People with limited mobility use Morse to communicate via eye blinks or muscle twitches
🎮 Escape Rooms Game designers love it. You will solve at least one Morse puzzle before you die

Beginners manage about 5 words per minute — roughly the speed of someone typing with oven mitts on.

Trained operators hit 20–30 WPM comfortably. Military experts pushed past 40 WPM. The world record? A jaw-dropping 75.2 WPM set by Ted McElroy in 1939, a record that stood for decades.

Nerd corner

At peak WPM, experienced operators stop consciously decoding individual letters — they hear whole words as single musical shapes, like how you recognise a song from the first two notes. The brain is wild.

The old method was memorising a chart. The modern method is way more fun:

1
Learn by sound, not by sight.
Each code has a rhythm. "Di-dah" = A. "Dah-di-di-dit" = B. Hear the music, not the dots.
2
Start with E, T, I, A, N, M.
These are the shortest codes. Get these six in your bones first.
3
Use the Trainer tab above.
Even 5 minutes a day builds the pattern recognition surprisingly fast.
4
Don't try to be fast early.
Accuracy first, speed later. Every expert started by going embarrassingly slow.
